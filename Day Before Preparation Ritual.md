The rule: No new learning tonight. Tonight is about retrieval, not acquisition. You already know what you know. The goal is to access it confidently under pressure.




Hour 1 — Technical consolidation (60 min)

Step 1 — Write your three best project stories in DARCM format (25 min)

For each of your top 3 projects, write one paragraph per DARCM component. Do this in writing — not in your head. The act of writing forces clarity you won't have under interview pressure.

Template:

**Decision:** We chose [X] over [Y] because [specific reason].

**Alternative rejected:** We evaluated [Y] and rejected it because [specific problem —

**Risk navigated:** The main risk was [specific risk]. We mitigated it by [specific action].

**Consequence at scale:** When [scale event happened], the system [behaved this way]. 
The failure mode we had designed around was [X].

**Measure:** [Specific metric] improved from [baseline] to [result], which meant 
[business outcome] for the client.

Hour 2 — Mindset and logistics (60 min)

Step 4 — Research the interviewer and client's AI context (20 min)


 Look up the interviewer on LinkedIn — their background, current focus, recent posts
 Check if they've written or spoken about a specific AI topic recently — know it
 Look up the company's recent AI news, product launches, or published case studies
 Prepare one specific, relevant observation to open with



High-value opening: "I noticed you recently announced [X] — I was curious about the architecture approach there." One sentence. Signals genuine preparation.



Step 5 — Prepare 3 questions to ask them (10 min)

Best closing questions for a Data & AI client interview:


"What does the AI system you're building need to do in 12 months that it can't do today?"
"What's the biggest technical challenge the team is wrestling with right now?"
"How mature is the data foundation — are we building on clean data or is remediation part of the scope?"



These questions signal commercial thinking and technical depth simultaneously. Avoid "what does the culture look like" in a technical interview — save it for the culture round.



Step 6 — Logistics check (10 min)


 Confirm interview time zone (critical for remote)
 Test video, audio, and screen share if virtual
 Have your three project stories on a single page (to glance at, not read from)
 Have a blank page ready for whiteboarding / architecture sketching
 Know the address and route if in-person — aim to arrive 15 minutes early


Step 7 — The confidence reset (20 min)

Stop preparing. Do something that makes you feel competent and calm — a short walk, a favourite playlist, a coffee.

The most damaging thing you can do in the final 20 minutes is keep cramming. Anxiety compounds with diminishing returns on learning. You are already prepared. The goal now is to walk in with your natural energy, not your nervous energy.


7. Morning-Of Anchors

Carry these four mental anchors into the interview:


Anchor 1 — You are evaluating them too

The interview is mutual. You are deciding if this client and this project are worth your time. That mindset shift removes desperation from your answers and makes you genuinely curious rather than performatively eager. Curiosity reads as confidence.


Anchor 2 — Decisions, not descriptions

Before every technical answer, ask yourself: "What decision am I going to lead with?" Not what did I build — what did I choose, what did I reject, and why. That discipline in every answer is what makes you sound like an architect.


Anchor 3 — Silence is allowed

A 3-second pause before answering a complex question signals that you're thinking carefully, not that you don't know.


"Let me make sure I give you a precise answer on that."



That sentence builds confidence, not erodes it. Rushing into a half-answer is far worse than pausing.


Anchor 4 — Bridge every gap, apologise for nothing

You will be asked about something you haven't used. When that happens, your only job is to bridge to what you do know. Apologising for the gap draws attention to it. Bridging from it demonstrates depth.

The candidate who bridges cleanly is more impressive than the one with a superficial "yes."
